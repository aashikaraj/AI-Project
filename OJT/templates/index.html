About Us

Welcome to FLOOD FORECASTER

A web app to help users and the government prepare for and reallocate resources for floods. Floods are one of the most dangerous and frequent natural disasters in the world.

Every year, thousands of people die from the floods and millions of people lose their homes and livelihoods. In many states around India and China's Yellow River Valley, some of the world's worst floods have killed thousands, if not millions of people. Our team wanted to help solve this problem, so we created FloodML to predict floods and their impact before they even happen. Then, we display this information in an interactive graphical format, making the information compelling and easy to understand for people and governments alike.

KNOW MORE ABOUT

Flood

It is important to know about floods and their consequences in India because they cause significant damage to lives, infrastructure, and the economy. Understanding the impact of floods helps in developing effective disaster risk reduction strategies, addressing social inequalities, and ensuring food security. It also enables policymakers to take proactive measures to mitigate the effects of floods.

  • Monsoon & Flood Safety in India
  • International Real Time Alerts
Monsoon Rains
India's monsoon season brings heavy rainfall, which can lead to rivers overflowing, especially in the plains. The southwest monsoon, in particular, contributes to the majority of the annual rainfall. When the monsoon is particularly intense or prolonged, it can cause widespread flooding in both urban and rural areas.
Dam and Embankment Failures
Dams and embankments are designed to control water flow, but failures can occur due to structural issues, poor maintenance, or excessive water release during heavy rains. When these structures fail, the sudden release of water can cause catastrophic flooding downstream, affecting large populations and agricultural lands.
River System Overflow
India's major rivers, such as the Ganges and Brahmaputra, are prone to overflowing during the monsoon season. Their extensive networks of tributaries and distributaries can exacerbate the situation, leading to flooding in floodplains and delta regions. The topography and high volume of water flow contribute significantly to riverine floods.
Cyclones and Storm Surges
Cyclones originating in the Bay of Bengal and Arabian Sea often bring heavy rainfall and strong winds, causing coastal flooding. Storm surges associated with these cyclones can raise sea levels, leading to the inundation of coastal areas. High tides during these events can worsen the flooding situation.
Climate Change
Climate change is altering rainfall patterns, leading to more intense and unpredictable monsoon seasons. The frequency of extreme weather events, such as heavy downpours and cyclones, is increasing, resulting in higher flood risks. Additionally, rising sea levels due to global warming contribute to coastal flooding, especially in low-lying areas.
Deforestation and Soil Erosion
Deforestation in catchment areas reduces the natural absorption of rainwater, increasing surface runoff and the likelihood of flooding. Soil erosion from deforested areas contributes to sedimentation in rivers, reducing their capacity to hold water and increasing the risk of floods. This is particularly prevalent in hilly regions where deforestation is rampant.
